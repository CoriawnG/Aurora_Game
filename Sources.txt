1. Advisor System & Decision Support

Sources / Citations:

Simon, H. A. (1977). The New Science of Management Decision. Prentice Hall.
Explains bounded rationality and expert input in organizational decisions.
Keen, P. G. W., & Scott Morton, M. S. (1978). Decision Support Systems: An Organizational Perspective. Addison-Wesley.
Foundation of decision support systems (DSS) where advice guides executive choices.
A real-world example: Governments use expert advisory councils to guide policy. For instance, the U.S. Council of Economic Advisers provides recommendations on fiscal and economic policies.
Source: U.S. Council of Economic Advisers. https://www.whitehouse.gov/cea/


2. Stats (Stability, Prosperity, Equality, Freedom)

Sources / Citations:

World Bank. (2023). World Development Indicators.
Tracks economic and social metrics similar to Prosperity and Equality. https://data.worldbank.org/
Freedom House. (2022). Freedom in the World 2022.
Measures political rights and civil liberties. Directly relates to your "Freedom" stat. https://freedomhouse.org/report/freedom-world
World Economic Forum. (2022). Global Competitiveness Report.
Stability, prosperity, and social cohesion metrics are core indicators for policy evaluation. https://www.weforum.org/reports/global-competitiveness-report-2022


3. Dynamic Events & Policy Consequences

Sources / Citations:

Meadows, D. H., Meadows, D. L., & Randers, J. (2004). Limits to Growth: The 30-Year Update. Chelsea Green Publishing.
Shows systems modeling where events affect multiple societal metrics.
Policy simulators in the real world:
NATO Crisis Management Simulations – simulate civil unrest, economy, and political decisions.
Reference: NATO Modeling & Simulation Centre of Excellence https://www.mccoe.org/
Game-based analog: Democracy series by Positech Games (2005–present).
Simulates policy decisions affecting national metrics (economy, stability, rights).


4. Advisor Trust & Reputation System

Sources / Citations:

Mayer, R. C., Davis, J. H., & Schoorman, F. D. (1995). An Integrative Model of Organizational Trust. Academy of Management Review, 20(3), 709–734.
Foundational research on trust dynamics in organizations.
AI simulation reference:
Gilbert, N., & Troitzsch, K. G. (2005). Simulation for the Social Scientist. McGraw-Hill.
Shows reputation models in agent-based simulations.
Real-world analog:
Central banks and advisory boards adjust recommendations based on the trustworthiness and historical accuracy of advisors.


5. Endings / Societal Outcomes

Sources / Citations:

Acemoglu, D., & Robinson, J. A. (2012). Why Nations Fail: The Origins of Power, Prosperity, and Poverty. Crown Business.
Discusses how policy decisions, institutions, and freedoms lead to societal success or collapse.
World Bank Governance Indicators (WGI) track the effects of policy on stability and prosperity.
https://info.worldbank.org/governance/wgi/
Simulation-based research in political science uses similar thresholds for system collapse or stability (see Axelrod’s The Evolution of Cooperation, 1984).

6. Game Design / Educational Application

Sources / Citations:

Squire, K. (2011). Video Games and Learning: Teaching and Participatory Culture in the Digital Age. Teachers College Press.
Explains how games model complex social systems for learning.
Gee, J. P. (2007). What Video Games Have to Teach Us About Learning and Literacy. Palgrave Macmillan.
Shows how player choices and consequences can teach systems thinking.
Example: SimCity and Civilization series – teach players how balancing economy, social services, and citizen happiness affects societal outcomes.
